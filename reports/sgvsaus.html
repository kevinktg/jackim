A Family's Crossroads

An Interactive Dashboard for Comparing Autism Support in Singapore and Australia

This tool translates a complex strategic report into an interactive experience. It moves beyond simple funding numbers to explore the core trade-off: Singapore's predictability versus Australia's flexibility—a choice now defined by the critical uncertainty of Australia's NDIS reforms.

The Early Years: A Clash of Models

Early intervention reveals the fundamental difference between the two systems. Singapore delivers a high volume of structured, programmatic support, while Australia provides a monetary budget for families to purchase services from an open market. This section visualizes the crucial distinction between service intensity and direct funding.

Singapore (EIPIC): Service Intensity

Singapore's state-guided EIPIC program focuses on delivering a high volume of structured, centre-based intervention hours per week at a heavily subsidized cost for citizens.

This chart highlights the sheer volume of programmed service hours delivered, a key strength of Singapore's structured model.

Australia (NDIS): Monetary Value

The NDIS provides a direct funding package. This visualization shows how an average early intervention budget translates into purchasable one-on-one therapy hours.

This demonstrates the model's focus on "choice and control" through purchasing power, but also reveals the potential cap on service intensity.

Navigating the School Years: Two Divergent Paths

During school years, the systems diverge completely. Singapore offers a clear, integrated choice between mainstream and specialized, low-cost SPED schools. Australia requires families to navigate two separate bureaucracies: the education system and the NDIS, creating a significant administrative burden.

CRITICAL RISK: The Future of the NDIS

The Australian NDIS is undergoing a fundamental, cost-driven overhaul. This is not a future possibility; it is happening now. The entire value proposition for migrating families, based on the 2021 landscape, is at risk.

The "Foundational Supports" Shift

A new, parallel system is being created for children with "mild to moderate" needs, explicitly designed to divert them away from the full NDIS from 2027 onwards.

The Risk of Ineligibility

A child who qualifies for a substantial NDIS package today may not be eligible for the same scheme after migrating, as criteria tighten. The definition of "mild to moderate" remains dangerously ambiguous.

The Final Verdict: A Strategic Checklist

The decision is not about which country is "better," but which system's risks and benefits align with your family's needs and risk tolerance. The stability of Singapore's system now stands in stark contrast to the uncertainty in Australia.

  • 1.

    Assess Your Appetite for Systemic Risk

    Are you comfortable relocating to a country in the midst of a fundamental overhaul of its flagship disability support system? Or do you prefer the stability of Singapore's state-managed pathway?

  • 2.

    Evaluate Your Child's Clinical Profile

    Critically assess if your child's needs would be classified as "severe and permanent" or "mild to moderate" under a stricter future NDIS framework. This is the key to potential eligibility.

  • 3.

    Model Your Financial Resilience

    Can you afford the "PR penalty" in Singapore? Do you have the capacity to cover a potential 50%+ funding gap in Australia if NDIS support is reduced or insufficient?

  • 4.

    Define Your Educational Preference

    Do you value the all-in-one, specialized environment of a SPED school, or the philosophy of mainstream inclusion, with its associated administrative burden?
